AKIRA is a sophisticated Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) operation that emerged in March 2023 and has rapidly established itself as one of the most dangerous and financially successful ransomware threats targeting organizations globally. The threat actor has demonstrated exceptional operational capabilities, sophisticated technical implementation, and continuous evolution of tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).

Threat Actor Name: AKIRA / Akira Ransomware Group
Associated Groups: Storm-1567, Howling Scorpius, PUNK SPIDER, GOLD SAHARA
Suspected Origin: Russia (linked to defunct Conti ransomware syndicate)
Operation Model: Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS)
Primary Motivation: Financial Gain

üë§ THREAT ACTOR PROFILE

Origin & Political Affiliation

AKIRA is believed to have strong connections to the now-defunct Conti ransomware syndicate, which ceased operations in May 2022 following the Russian invasion of Ukraine and subsequent internal conflicts. Multiple indicators support this connection:

  • Code Overlap: Technical analysis reveals significant similarities between AKIRA and Conti ransomware, including identical file type exclusions, similar directory targeting, and comparable encryption implementation
  • Blockchain Analysis: Cryptocurrency transaction analysis has identified financial links between AKIRA wallet addresses and known Conti-affiliated wallets
  • Operational Similarities: AKIRA employs tactics and techniques consistent with known Conti operations
  • Geographic Indicators: While AKIRA targets organizations globally, patterns suggest Russian-speaking operators

Political Affiliation: No explicit political agenda has been identified. AKIRA appears to be purely financially motivated, avoiding attribution to any state-sponsored activities. The group operates as a criminal enterprise focused on maximum profit extraction through ransomware operations.

Typical Victims & Target Selection

AKIRA demonstrates an opportunistic yet strategic victim selection process:

Geographic Targeting:

  • Primary: United States (largest victim demographic - approximately 80% of victims)
  • Secondary: Canada, United Kingdom, Germany, Australia, France
  • Emerging: Latin America (recent expansion observed)
  • Coverage: North America, Europe, Asia-Pacific regions

Industry Sectors Targeted:

  • Manufacturing (most heavily targeted)
  • Healthcare and Public Health
  • Education (K-12 and Higher Education)
  • Financial Services
  • Professional Services
  • Construction and Materials
  • Information Technology
  • Critical Infrastructure
  • Government Organizations
  • Retail and Consumer Services

Organization Size Profile:

  • Small Businesses: 1-200 employees (59% of victims)
  • Medium Enterprises: 200-1,000 employees (significant portion)
  • Large Organizations: 1,000+ employees (selective targeting)

AKIRA is assessed to be an opportunistic threat actor that targets organizations of all sizes, with particular focus on small-to-medium businesses (SMBs) that often have limited cybersecurity resources but maintain valuable data assets.

Typical Cybercrime Activities

üîê Ransomware Operations (Primary Activity)

AKIRA's core business model revolves around sophisticated ransomware deployment targeting Windows, Linux, and VMware ESXi environments. The group has developed multiple ransomware variants including the original C++-based version, the Rust-based "Megazord" variant, and "Akira_v2" ESXi encryptor.

üíæ Data Exfiltration & Double Extortion

Prior to encryption, AKIRA systematically exfiltrates sensitive organizational data including:

  • Customer databases and personal information (PII)
  • Financial records and banking information
  • Intellectual property and trade secrets
  • Internal communications and sensitive documents
  • Contract agreements and legal documents
  • Employee data and HR records

The stolen data is leveraged in a double-extortion model where victims face two separate threats:

  1. Operational Disruption: Encrypted systems preventing business operations
  2. Data Publication: Public release of sensitive data on dark web leak site if ransom is not paid

Uniquely, AKIRA offers victims flexibility in choosing what to pay for - either file decryption, data deletion, or both - with ransom demands ranging from $200,000 to over $4,000,000 based on victim organization size and perceived ability to pay.

üé≠ Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) Operations

AKIRA operates a centralized RaaS platform where:

  • Core group maintains ransomware development and infrastructure
  • Affiliates conduct individual attacks using provided tools and infrastructure
  • Revenue sharing arrangement between operators and affiliates
  • Centralized negotiation portal and leak site on Tor network
  • Coordinated brand management and public communications

üåê Infrastructure Compromise & Persistence

Beyond immediate ransomware deployment, AKIRA engages in:

  • Long-term network compromise for reconnaissance
  • Credential harvesting and privilege escalation
  • Lateral movement across enterprise networks
  • Backup destruction to prevent recovery
  • Security tool disablement and anti-forensic techniques

Notable Characteristics:

  • Speed of Attack: Capable of progressing from initial access to data exfiltration in as little as 2 hours
  • Retro Aesthetic: Tor-based leak site styled after 1980s "green screen" terminals, navigable via command-line interface
  • Victim Engagement: Provides "decryption proof" samples to build trust during negotiations
  • Active Management: Operators actively manage attack lifecycle, demonstrating sophistication and adaptability
  • Continuous Evolution: Regular updates to encryption methods, target platforms, and evasion techniques

Description: AKIRA operates a double-extortion ransomware model focused on financial gain through ransom payments and threat of data publication.

  • Secondary Threat: Public release of stolen data on Tor-based leak site if payment not made

  • Negotiation Process: Conducted through dedicated Tor portal with unique victim codes
  • Payment Method: Bitcoin to cryptocurrency wallet addresses provided during negotiation

  • Proof of Capability: Provision of sample file decryption to build trust

Financial Impact: As of September 2025, AKIRA has extorted approximately $244.17 million USD from over 350 confirmed victims worldwide.

üõ†Ô∏è TOOLS & UTILITIES

AKIRA threat actors leverage a sophisticated toolkit combining legitimate administrative tools, open-source utilities, and custom malware to accomplish their objectives across all attack phases:

Remote Access & Persistence Tools

AnyDesk

Category: Legitimate Remote Desktop Software

Malicious Use: AKIRA extensively uses AnyDesk to maintain persistent remote access to compromised systems. Once deployed, threat actors can control systems, transfer files, and execute commands without triggering alerts for unauthorized remote access tools.

Detection Indicators: Unexpected AnyDesk installations, connections to unusual external IPs, execution from non-standard directories

RustDesk

Category: Open-Source Remote Desktop

Malicious Use: Alternative to AnyDesk for maintaining backdoor access. RustDesk's open-source nature allows threat actors to customize and deploy without licensing concerns.

MobaXterm

Category: Enhanced Terminal with SSH/RDP

Malicious Use: Used for lateral movement via SSH and RDP connections. MobaXterm provides integrated tools for file transfer and remote execution.

LogMeIn

Category: Commercial Remote Access

Malicious Use: Leveraged for persistent access and lateral movement within compromised networks.

Radmin

Category: Remote Administration Tool

Malicious Use: Remote control and file transfer capabilities used for maintaining access and moving laterally.

Credential Access Tools

Mimikatz

Category: Credential Extraction Tool

Malicious Use: Industry-standard tool for dumping credentials from LSASS memory, extracting Kerberos tickets, and performing pass-the-hash attacks. AKIRA uses Mimikatz extensively for privilege escalation and lateral movement.

Capabilities Exploited:

  • LSASS memory credential dumping
  • Kerberos ticket extraction (Golden/Silver tickets)
  • Pass-the-hash and pass-the-ticket attacks
  • Windows Credential Manager access
  • DCSync attacks against domain controllers

LaZagne

Category: Password Recovery Tool

Malicious Use: Open-source credential harvesting tool that retrieves stored passwords from multiple sources including browsers, email clients, databases, WiFi networks, and system vaults.

Targeted Credential Stores:

  • Web browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Edge)
  • Email clients (Outlook, Thunderbird)
  • FTP clients (FileZilla, WinSCP)
  • Windows Credential Manager
  • Database credentials (MySQL, PostgreSQL)

DonPAPI

Category: DPAPI Credential Extraction

Malicious Use: Specialized tool for extracting credentials protected by Windows Data Protection API (DPAPI), including browser credentials and Windows Vault data.

SharpDomainSpray

Category: Password Spraying Tool

Malicious Use: Performs password spraying attacks against Active Directory accounts to identify weak credentials without triggering account lockouts.

Discovery & Reconnaissance Tools

AdFind

Category: Active Directory Query Tool

Malicious Use: Command-line utility for querying and extracting detailed information from Active Directory including user accounts, computers, groups, organizational units, and trust relationships.

# Common AdFind commands used by AKIRA AdFind.exe -f "(objectcategory=person)" AdFind.exe -f "(objectcategory=computer)" AdFind.exe -sc trustdmp AdFind.exe -subnets -f (objectCategory=subnet)

Advanced IP Scanner

Category: Network Scanner

Malicious Use: Fast network scanning tool used to identify all devices on a network, discover shared folders, and enable remote control via RDP. AKIRA uses this for comprehensive network mapping.

SoftPerfect Network Scanner (netscan.exe)

Category: Multi-Protocol Network Scanner

Malicious Use: Advanced scanning capabilities including port scanning, NetBIOS information retrieval, WMI queries, SNMP data collection, and remote service discovery.

MASSCAN

Category: High-Speed Port Scanner

Malicious Use: Ultra-fast TCP port scanner used to quickly identify open ports and services across large network ranges.

NetScan

Category: Network Analysis Tool

Malicious Use: IP querying, DNS investigation, and port scanning for reconnaissance.

PCHunter64

Category: System Information Tool

Malicious Use: Deep system inspection tool used to gather detailed process, service, driver, and registry information.

SharpHound

Category: Active Directory Collector (BloodHound)

Malicious Use: Automated collection of Active Directory relationships and permissions for attack path analysis.

Data Exfiltration Tools

Rclone

Category: Cloud Storage Sync Tool

Malicious Use: Legitimate tool repurposed for exfiltrating stolen data to cloud storage services (Mega, Amazon S3, Google Drive). Rclone's encryption capabilities and support for 40+ cloud providers make it ideal for covert data exfiltration.

Common Configuration: AKIRA configures Rclone to sync data to attacker-controlled cloud accounts, often using encrypted transfers to evade DLP solutions.

FileZilla

Category: FTP Client

Malicious Use: File transfer via FTP/SFTP to attacker-controlled servers. FileZilla's GUI and saved session capabilities make it convenient for batch file transfers.

WinSCP

Category: SCP/SFTP Client

Malicious Use: Secure file transfer protocol client used to exfiltrate data over SSH connections. Supports scripting for automated transfers.

WinRAR / 7-Zip

Category: Archive Utilities

Malicious Use: Compression and archiving of stolen data prior to exfiltration. AKIRA typically:

  • Compresses data to reduce transfer time and bandwidth usage
  • Splits large archives into smaller segments for easier exfiltration
  • Uses password-protected archives to evade content inspection
  • Archives data in RAR or 7z formats

Command & Control Tools

Ngrok

Category: Reverse Proxy / Tunneling Service

Malicious Use: Creates encrypted tunnels from compromised systems to external command-and-control servers, bypassing firewall restrictions and hiding malicious traffic within HTTPS. Enables secure remote access and data exfiltration channels.

Cloudflare Tunnel (cloudflared.exe)

Category: Secure Tunneling Service

Malicious Use: Alternative to Ngrok for establishing encrypted C2 channels that appear as legitimate Cloudflare traffic.

# Example Cloudflare Tunnel command C:\ProgramData\cloudflared.exe tunnel run --token [REDACTED]

SystemBC

Category: Multi-Purpose Malware

Malicious Use: Used as both a Remote Access Trojan (RAT) and SOCKS5 proxy bot for command and control communications.

Cobalt Strike

Category: Commercial Penetration Testing Framework

Malicious Use: Industry-standard adversary simulation tool repurposed for malicious operations including lateral movement, privilege escalation, and maintaining persistent access.

Defense Evasion Tools

PowerTool

Category: Process Termination Tool

Malicious Use: Exploits the Zemana AntiMalware driver to terminate security processes with elevated privileges. Used to disable antivirus, EDR, and other security software before encryption.

# PowerTool used to kill security processes PowerTool.exe /kill [AV_Process_PID]

POORTRY

Category: Malicious Kernel Driver

Malicious Use: Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver (BYOVD) technique - signed vulnerable Windows driver used to obtain kernel-level privileges and disable security software.

STONESTOP

HeartCrypt

Category: Packer-as-a-Service

Malicious Use: Obfuscation tool used to pack and encrypt payloads, hindering static analysis and evading signature-based detection.

Terminator / KillAV

Category: Security Software Killer

Malicious Use: Tools specifically designed to terminate antivirus and EDR processes.

Lateral Movement Tools

PsExec

Category: Sysinternals Remote Execution Tool

Malicious Use: Legitimate Microsoft Sysinternals utility used for remote command execution and lateral movement across Windows networks.

Impacket

Category: Python Network Protocol Library

Malicious Use: Python library providing implementations of network protocols. AKIRA uses Impacket's wmiexec.py, atexec.py, and smbexec.py for remote command execution and lateral movement.

Backup & Recovery Exploitation

AKIRA threat actors systematically exploit known vulnerabilities in public-facing applications and network devices to gain initial access. The following CVEs represent the most critical vulnerabilities actively exploited by AKIRA:

Critical - Cisco VPN Vulnerabilities

CVE-2020-3259 (CVSS 9.8 - Critical)

Vulnerability: Information Disclosure in Cisco ASA Software and FTD Software

Affected Products: Cisco Adaptive Security Appliance (ASA) Software, Cisco Firepower Threat Defense (FTD) Software

Description: A vulnerability in the web services interface allows an unauthenticated, remote attacker to access sensitive session information, potentially leading to full VPN credential compromise.

AKIRA Exploitation: Heavily exploited for initial access to corporate networks via VPN. One of the most commonly observed CVEs in AKIRA incidents.

Impact: Unauthenticated remote attackers can obtain session tokens and authenticate as legitimate users.

CWE: CWE-200 (Exposure of Sensitive Information to an Unauthorized Actor)

CVE-2023-20269 (CVSS 9.8 - Critical)

Vulnerability: Authentication Bypass in Cisco ASA and FTD Software Web Services

Affected Products: Cisco ASA Software, Cisco FTD Software

Description: Allows an unauthenticated, remote attacker to conduct a brute force attack in an attempt to identify valid username and password combinations or to conduct a username enumeration attack.

AKIRA Exploitation: Used in conjunction with brute force attacks to compromise VPN credentials without multi-factor authentication.

Impact: Complete authentication bypass enabling unauthorized VPN access.

CWE: CWE-288 (Authentication Bypass Using an Alternate Path or Channel)

CVE-2020-3580 (CVSS 6.1 - Medium)

Vulnerability: Cross-Site Scripting (XSS) in Cisco ASA and FTD Software

Affected Products: Cisco ASA Software, Cisco FTD Software

Description: Multiple cross-site scripting vulnerabilities in the web-based management interface that could allow an unauthenticated, remote attacker to conduct XSS attacks.

AKIRA Exploitation: Used to steal session cookies and administrative credentials.

CWE: CWE-79 (Improper Neutralization of Input During Web Page Generation)

CVE-2024-37085 (CVSS 8.6 - High)

Vulnerability: Authentication Bypass in Cisco ASA and FTD Software

Affected Products: Cisco ASA Software, Cisco FTD Software (newer versions)

Description: Vulnerability in the web services interface allows an unauthenticated remote attacker to bypass authentication.

AKIRA Exploitation: Recently added to AKIRA's arsenal for compromising updated Cisco devices.

Impact: Complete authentication bypass for web services interface.

CWE: CWE-305 (Authentication Bypass by Primary Weakness)

Critical - SonicWall VPN Vulnerabilities

CVE-2024-40766 (CVSS 9.3 - Critical)

Vulnerability: Improper Access Control in SonicWall SonicOS

Affected Products: SonicWall SSL VPN SonicOS

Description: Critical access control vulnerability allowing unauthorized access to SSL VPN services.

AKIRA Exploitation: Heavily exploited in 2024-2025 campaigns. AKIRA has successfully used this vulnerability to:

  • Gain initial access to networks without valid credentials
  • Bypass authentication mechanisms
  • Establish persistent VPN connections
  • In one June 2025 incident, encrypt Nutanix AHV VM disk files

Impact: Complete network compromise via VPN access.

CWE: CWE-284 (Improper Access Control)

Status: Actively exploited in the wild by AKIRA

Affected Products: Veeam Backup & Replication (versions prior to 12.0.0.1420)

Behavioral Indicators

  • Rapid Lateral Movement: RDP/SSH connections across multiple hosts within short timeframes
  • Mass File Access: Single account accessing large numbers of files across network shares
  • Off-Hours Activity: Administrative activity during non-business hours
  • Credential Anomalies: Use of dormant or vendor accounts, privilege escalation attempts
  • Backup Targeting: Access to backup servers, deletion of backup catalogs
  • Tool Staging: Multiple administrative tools appearing in PerfLogs, Temp, or ProgramData directories
  • Process Injection: PowerShell or cmd.exe spawning from unusual parent processes
  • Security Software Tampering: Antivirus/EDR services stopped or disabled

Detection Opportunities

SIEM/Log Analysis Focus Areas:

  • Failed VPN login attempts followed by successful authentication
  • New local or domain admin account creation
  • PowerShell execution with suspicious parameters (IEX, DownloadString, -EncodedCommand)
  • LSASS memory access by non-system processes
  • Volume Shadow Copy deletion events (Event ID 7036, 521, 524)
  • Windows Defender real-time protection disabled (Event ID 5001)
  • Remote access tool installations (AnyDesk, RustDesk)
  • Large data transfers to external IP addresses
  • Multiple service stop events in short succession

Comprehensive security measures to prevent, detect, and respond to AKIRA ransomware attacks. These recommendations are based on observed AKIRA TTPs and align with industry best practices and CISA Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs).

üîí Priority 1: Critical Security Controls

1. Vulnerability Management & Patching

Immediate Actions Required:

  • Patch all Cisco ASA/FTD devices (CVE-2020-3259, CVE-2023-20269, CVE-2024-37085)
  • Update SonicWall SonicOS immediately (CVE-2024-40766)
  • Upgrade Veeam Backup & Replication to version 12.0.0.1420 or later
  • Apply all Windows security updates, especially CLFS driver patches
  • Review and patch all internet-facing systems within 14 days of disclosure
  • Implement automated vulnerability scanning and patch management
  • Prioritize patching systems listed in CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog

2. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Implementation Requirements:

  • Mandatory MFA on ALL remote access: VPN, RDP, SSH, remote management interfaces
  • Phishing-resistant MFA preferred: Hardware tokens (YubiKey), FIDO2, certificate-based authentication
  • Avoid SMS/Phone-based MFA: Vulnerable to SIM-swapping and interception
  • Enforce MFA for all privileged accounts (administrators, service accounts)
  • Implement MFA for cloud services (Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, AWS, Azure)
  • Do NOT allow MFA bypass or "remember this device" for extended periods
  • Monitor and alert on MFA fatigue attacks (repeated push notifications)

3. Backup Strategy (3-2-1-1 Rule)

Comprehensive Backup Framework:

  • 3 copies of critical data
  • 2 different storage media types
  • 1 copy stored offsite
  • 1 copy offline (air-gapped) and immutable

Critical Implementation Details:

  • Implement immutable backups that cannot be deleted or modified for defined retention periods
  • Store backups offline or in isolated network segments inaccessible from production
  • Encrypt all backup data with strong encryption (AES-256)
  • Test backup restoration regularly (monthly at minimum)
  • Document and drill complete disaster recovery procedures
  • Protect backup credentials separately from production credentials
  • Monitor backup systems for unauthorized access or modification attempts
  • Implement role-based access control (RBAC) for backup administration

üîê Access Control & Account Management

Principle of Least Privilege

  • Limit administrative privileges to only those who absolutely require them
  • Implement Just-In-Time (JIT) access for administrative tasks
  • Use separate accounts for administrative tasks (no dual-use accounts)
  • Regularly audit and remove unnecessary permissions
  • Disable or remove inactive accounts (30-day threshold)
  • Implement Privileged Access Workstations (PAWs) for administrative tasks

Password Policy

  • Require passwords of at least 15 characters (20+ for privileged accounts)
  • Implement password complexity requirements
  • Use enterprise password managers for credential storage
  • Avoid requiring frequent password changes (annual maximum)
  • Implement account lockout after 5 failed login attempts
  • Monitor for password spraying and credential stuffing attacks
  • Check passwords against known breach databases

üåê Network Segmentation & Architecture

Network Isolation Strategies

  • Segment critical systems (domain controllers, backup servers, financial systems)
  • Implement VLANs to separate network traffic by function/sensitivity
  • Deploy internal firewalls between network segments
  • Restrict lateral movement with host-based firewalls
  • Isolate OT/ICS networks from IT networks
  • Implement Zero Trust Network Architecture (ZTNA) principles
  • Require authentication for all internal communications
  • Deploy network access control (NAC) solutions

üëÅÔ∏è Detection & Monitoring

Endpoint Detection & Response (EDR)

  • Deploy EDR solutions on all endpoints (workstations, servers)
  • Enable behavioral analysis and machine learning detection
  • Monitor for LSASS memory access, credential dumping attempts
  • Alert on PowerShell execution with suspicious parameters
  • Detect and block BYOVD (Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver) attacks
  • Implement application whitelisting/allowlisting
  • Monitor for suspicious process creation chains

Security Information & Event Management (SIEM)

  • Centralize log collection from all critical systems
  • Retain logs for minimum 90 days (365+ days for compliance)
  • Implement real-time alerting for critical events:
    • New admin account creation
    • Failed login attempts followed by success
    • Service stop events (especially backup/security services)
    • Volume Shadow Copy deletion
    • Windows Defender disabled events
    • Mass file access/modification
    • Unusual outbound connections
  • Correlate events across multiple systems to detect attack patterns
  • Tune detection rules to minimize false positives
  • Establish baseline behavior for anomaly detection

Network Traffic Monitoring

  • Deploy network traffic analysis (NTA) solutions
  • Monitor for data exfiltration (large outbound transfers)
  • Block or alert on Tor network connections from corporate networks
  • Inspect encrypted traffic where possible (TLS inspection)
  • Monitor for tunneling protocols (Ngrok, Cloudflare Tunnel)
  • Implement DNS filtering and monitoring
  • Alert on communications with known malicious IPs/domains

üö´ Prevention & Hardening

Email Security

  • Implement advanced email filtering (anti-phishing, anti-malware)
  • Add external email banners to warn users
  • Disable hyperlinks in emails from external senders
  • Block dangerous file types (.exe, .scr, .vbs, .js, .bat)
  • Implement DMARC, SPF, and DKIM for email authentication
  • Conduct regular phishing simulation training
  • Enable "Safe Links" and "Safe Attachments" features

Endpoint Hardening

  • Disable PowerShell for standard users (or use Constrained Language Mode)
  • Disable Windows Script Host (WSH) for non-administrative users
  • Disable macros by default in Office applications
  • Enable Windows Defender Attack Surface Reduction (ASR) rules
  • Enable Credential Guard on Windows 10/11 systems
  • Disable LLMNR and NetBIOS to prevent credential harvesting
  • Configure Windows Firewall to block inbound RDP from internet
  • Remove local administrator rights from standard users
  • Disable unnecessary services and ports

Remote Access Security

  • Never expose RDP directly to the internet
  • Implement VPN with MFA for all remote access
  • Use VPN split-tunneling carefully (full tunnel preferred)
  • Implement geofencing - restrict VPN access by geographic location
  • Monitor VPN logs for suspicious activity
  • Implement conditional access policies
  • Use RDP Gateway or jump servers for internal RDP access
  • Require Network Level Authentication (NLA) for RDP

üìã Incident Response Preparation

Incident Response Plan

  • Develop and document comprehensive incident response procedures
  • Establish incident response team with defined roles and responsibilities
  • Maintain updated contact list (internal team, vendors, law enforcement, cyber insurance)
  • Conduct tabletop exercises quarterly
  • Pre-position incident response tools and access
  • Document and maintain system architecture and critical asset inventory
  • Establish communication protocols (internal and external)
  • Engage legal counsel familiar with cyber incidents

Ransomware-Specific Preparedness

  • Identify and document critical business systems and recovery priorities
  • Establish Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO)
  • Pre-position offline system images and installation media
  • Maintain offline copies of critical documentation and procedures
  • Test isolation procedures (can you quickly segment infected systems?)
  • Understand your cyber insurance coverage and requirements
  • Establish relationships with incident response firms before you need them
  • Do NOT engage with ransomware operators without proper guidance

‚ö†Ô∏è If Compromised - Immediate Actions

DO NOT PANIC - Follow These Steps:

  1. Isolate (Don't Power Off): Disconnect infected systems from network, but keep powered on for forensics
  2. Activate Incident Response: Contact your incident response team/vendor immediately
  3. Preserve Evidence: Take memory dumps, capture logs, document what you observe
  4. Notify Stakeholders: Executive leadership, legal, cyber insurance, law enforcement (FBI)
  5. Do NOT Pay Ransom: Without consulting legal counsel, law enforcement, and incident response experts
  6. Assess Scope: Determine extent of compromise before restoration
  7. Change Credentials: Reset all passwords, especially privileged accounts (after removing threat actor access)
  8. Report to Authorities: FBI IC3, CISA (critical infrastructure sectors)
